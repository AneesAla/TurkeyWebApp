Immediate Aftermath of the 2023 Turkey Earthquake

The 2023 earthquake in Turkey was a seismic event of unprecedented scale, impacting millions and exposing the critical need for urban planning and disaster readiness. The quake not only shattered lives but also infrastructure, leaving a lasting imprint on the region's landscape and its people's hearts.

The initial hours following the earthquake were marked by chaos and confusion as emergency services scrambled to respond amidst the widespread devastation. With many areas rendered inaccessible due to debris and damaged infrastructure, the race against time to rescue trapped survivors was fraught with difficulties. The community's resilience was tested as thousands of volunteers joined professional rescue teams, braving aftershocks and harsh conditions to search for signs of life beneath the rubble. This collective effort underscored the spirit of unity and the critical importance of timely and coordinated disaster response strategies.

Impact on the Communities

Burak: His home collapsed and buried him and his family under the debris, leaving them to the streets in bitter cold temperatures. He lost ten people in his family including a two-month-old baby. They were unable to escape after being buried shortly after the earthquake began. Burak spent more than an hour digging his way out until he could finally help his other family members out of the wreckage. Burak continued to help search for others and witnessed many who lost their lives. He was able to send his sister to other family relatives; however, his parents struggled to leave their beloved city of Adiyaman. He hopes to return to his job as a mariner to gather enough funds to rent, or perhaps one day even buy a new home.

Irmine [3rd from right]: Following the destruction of her home, this family has taken shelter in a center in Sanliurfa with access to food, water, heating, and other supplies. The earthquake instilled fear and trauma across thousands, making people weary of entering any concrete buildings. Irmine and her family felt safe at this center because of its internal steel structure. She occupies this space with thousands of others to avoid the cold conditions outside. They feel lucky for the basic necessities they currently have as many citizens are in far worse conditions. Irmine does not know how long they will stay here, but she knows it cannot last forever. This is currently their best option.

President's Promise

Recep Tayyip Erdoğan, the current president of Turkey since 2014, promised to “rebuild these buildings within one year and will hand them back to citizens.” He vowed to construct 650,000 new homes with 319,000 homes built within the first year and another 331,000 in the year after. Building efforts are slow, and this promise remains unfulfilled a year after the earthquake took place. Erdoğan’s promises are difficult to see as attainable by many amidst Turkey’s complicated economic challenges such as increasing inflation and cost of materials needed for infrastructure.

Balancing Act: The Environmental Challenges of Rapid Reconstruction Efforts

Reconstruction efforts that are this rapid can pose many risks such as increased carbon emissions, poor air quality, loss of biodiversity, inefficient land use, increased use of energy, and limited access to public spaces.
